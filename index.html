Amanda Pogue

Hi! I'm a fourth year graduate student in the Department of Brain and Cognitive Sciences at the University of Rochester. I'm presently advised by Michael Tanenhaus and Chigusa Kurumada, and can be found in the KurTan, Kinder, and HLP Labs! I'm interested in the kinds of information people take into account when interpreting the utterances of others. In particular my research asks questions about how people construct referential or communicative expectations, how they resolve conflicts when they receive unexpected evidence, and how they learn to do this from childhood.

Projects

Talker-Specific Adaptation

There is a lot of variability in the way that different people talk. As listeners we must try to navigate this variability to discover the intentions of our interlocutor. However, when we get evidence that is unexpected, how do we resolve the speaker's intention? In this line of research we ask whether listeners can track differences in informative referential expressions, and whether they consider some kinds of utterances to be characteristic of specific speakers. We also ask what kind of evidence might strengthen these generalizations.

Pragmatic Expectations in Sentence Processing

There are many ways in which a speaker can refer to things in the world, but when given evidence about a speaker's referential selections, do listeners update their expectations for other potential referents in a scene?
